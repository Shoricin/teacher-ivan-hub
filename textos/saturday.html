While humanity has mapped the entire surface of the Moon and Mars in high resolution, the deep ocean remains largely mysterious. In fact, scientists estimate that we have explored less of the ocean floor than we have of the surface of Mars. The primary obstacle preventing human exploration in the abyss is the extreme water pressure, which can easily crush conventional submarines.

The deep sea is a realm of eternal darkness, yet it teems with life. To survive in an environment devoid of sunlight, many marine creatures have evolved fascinating adaptations. One such adaptation is bioluminescence, a chemical process that allows animals to produce their own light. Deep-sea predators frequently use this glowing light as a lure to attract unsuspecting prey in the dark.

Because sending humans to these depths is incredibly dangerous and expensive, modern oceanographers rely heavily on technology. Today's deep-sea expeditions are conducted using advanced robotic submarines. These autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs) are capable of descending into the deepest trenches, collecting soil samples, and mapping the unexplored seafloor without risking human lives.
