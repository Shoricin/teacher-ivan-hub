Before the invention of formal alphabets, early human civilizations communicated and kept records using rudimentary methods. In ancient Mesopotamia, the Sumerians developed cuneiform script around 3400 BC. However, long before cuneiform, societies relied heavily on pictograms—simple drawings representing physical objects or concepts.

The primary driver for the evolution of writing was not storytelling or literature, but economics. As populations grew and cities formed, administrators needed a reliable way to keep track of grain storage, livestock, and most importantly, taxation. The rudimentary pictograms evolved into more abstract symbols to record these complex transactions.

Eventually, this need for efficiency led to the creation of the first phonemic writing systems. The Phoenicians are widely credited with inventing the first true alphabet around 1050 BC. Unlike previous systems that used thousands of symbols for entire words, this alphabet used a small set of characters to represent individual sounds, revolutionizing human communication forever.
