Microplastics, generally defined as plastic fragments measuring less than 5 millimeters in length, have become one of the most pervasive pollutants in the world's oceans. These tiny particles originate from a variety of sources, ranging from the breakdown of larger plastic debris like bottles and bags, to the microfibers shed during the washing of synthetic clothing.

The impact of microplastics on marine life is devastating. Because of their small size, these plastics are easily ingested by a wide range of marine species, from zooplankton to massive baleen whales. Studies have shown that many marine animals consume these plastics simply because they mistake them for their natural food sources, such as fish eggs or small crustaceans.

Once ingested, the danger multiplies. Microplastics are known to absorb toxic chemicals from the surrounding seawater, acting like miniature toxic sponges. When small fish eat these polluted particles, the toxins accumulate in their tissues. As larger predators consume the smaller fish, these concentrated toxins travel steadily up the food chain. Consequently, this poses a severe indirect threat to human health, particularly through the risk of eating contaminated seafood.
